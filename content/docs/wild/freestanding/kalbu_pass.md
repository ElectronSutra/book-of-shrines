Kalbu Pass is a narrow crag which cuts through the mountains north of Riven
Quay. The village of Kalbu is at the south end of the crag, and a path
just wide enough for a pair of donkeys leads up from the village through the
crag and to the other side of the mountains. It takes about a day's hike to
get all the way through the crag. Halfway through, a narrow cut in the crag's
walls lead up to a relatively flat natural overhang. This little platform is a
perfect place to rest, recuperate, and warm up as you face the rest of the hike.

## Physical description

The shrine itself is a natural platform large enough for setting up a
campsite. At the south end of the platform is another rise, on top of which is
a brass brazier dish, large enough for a small bonfire. The dish is elevated
from the stone on three metal legs that have been driven into the rock; if the
shrine has been visited by the locals recently, you may even find it stocked
up with wood ready to burn.

The center of the platform, immediately up the stairs, is a flat space
large enough to make camp for a small party and their animals -- if you can
get them up the stairs, that is.

The north side of the platform is home to the shrine altar itself. It is a
simple wooden structure, little more than a roof, four poles, and a base. The
base is carved with a dragon's head, once painted but now only flecked with
red. Atop the base is a wooden statuette, about the size of a man's torso, of an
eight-spoked flaming wheel: the mark of Hesiesh. The lineage stone is a simple
smooth stone from the river that runs through Kalbu, hand-carved with a
five-pointed star, in front of the statuette.

A wooden signpost is set in the rock next to the altar. It has a hook to hang a
lantern off of, the wooden sign is faded but can still be clearly read:

> KALBU PASS SHRINE
>
> LORD HESIESH PROTECTS AGAINST EVIL.
>
> LORD HESIESH OFFERS RESPITE FROM THE MOUNTAIN COLD.

The northwest edge of the platform is covered in handprints. Some are made
with ochre red, some charcoal black, others are faded colors. They range in
size from children's hands to a mark so large it surely must have come from
some giant or inhuman creature. The handprints trail off to the edge of the
platform; beneath, several hanging streamer-ropes in the five colors are
tattered but flap in the breeze, sometimes snapping with the mountain wind.

## Goings-on

Locals from Kalbu use the shrine as a resting place while hiking through the
gorge; seasonally, shepherds and hunters will cross to the other side of the
pass for grazing goats or seeking after deer and game. The shrine itself is
small and sleepy. If you investigate around the shrine, you will quickly find
a niche in the rock filled with bottles of rotgut, certainly stored by some
eager travelers from Kalbu who wanted to have some fun while they made camp.

The bottles are actually the property of Ossilbu, a dispossessed former bandit
cast out from Kalbu and now living in the wilderness at the far side of the
gorge. If encountered, Ossilbu will try to get away, but the only way down
safely from the platform is the stairs; that makes it easy to corner and
question him. He is a pathetic, filthy, skinny man with a long, wild beard and missing
several fingers. He claims he comes to the shrine to drink, to steal firewood,
and to seek the comforting presence of Lord Hesiesh. If pressed, he will reveal
his status as an outcast and as a former bandit, but he is pious enough not to
try to rob anyone literally at the shrine.

Ossilbu is a liar. He is indeed a former bandit, but he was not actually cast
out for that; he was cast out of Kalbu for his impropritious skill at
spirit-calling and his dealings with the sundry gods of the gorge. Ashamed and
seeking to return to society, Ossilbu prays at the shrine for repentence, and
his second great heresy is that he often believes he speaks directly to a
spirit of Hesiesh.

Ossilbu is loathe to discuss his spirit-calling skills, but may be persuaded by
food, drink, and kind words. He will confide that the local gods and elementals
are deeply disquieted and claim that there has been a deep darkness looming in
his mind. On the far side of the gorge are the burned relics of a town; nobody
in Kalbu speaks of them, and the cause of the fire was mysterious. Lanky
Ossilbu will say that he fears the unquiet dead have been uncovered in the
recent rains, and that the local spirits are unwilling to go near it.
